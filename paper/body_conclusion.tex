\section{Conclusion}

\textbf{WhiteTiger} is a billion-frame, fully human-teleoperated, multi-platform robot manipulation dataset for embodied foundation model adaptation and event-aware robot learning. The dataset contains \textbf{9521.75 hours} of robot data, \textbf{2989 tasks}, \textbf{513575 episodes}, and \textbf{1028349814 frames} across \textbf{13 robot platforms}. Platform-specific HDF5 operation records are converted into LeRobot v2.1, providing a standardized training interface while preserving embodiment-specific observation, state, action, and camera schemas.

WhiteTiger couples physical demonstration scale with temporal structure. Dense trajectories support foundation-model adaptation under real robot kinematics, sensing layouts, contact dynamics, and end-effector constraints. Event- and keyframe-level annotations expose task progress and semantic milestones beyond gripper or dexterous-hand state changes, including alignment, boundary crossing, handover, deformation, and object-state transitions. This structure enables research on event-conditioned imitation, hierarchical skill decomposition, progress-aware value learning, future-state prediction, and phase-aware evaluation.

Offline open-loop zero-shot evaluation on external datasets excluded from WhiteTiger training measures the dataset's utility for action-prediction transfer. Across 13 platforms and 42 paired task-dataset records, one epoch of full fine-tuning on WhiteTiger reduces \textbf{Normalized ALL MSE by 96.79\%} and \textbf{Normalized Joint MSE by 99.42\%} relative to a no-training GR00T N1.6 baseline. The result establishes WhiteTiger as a large-scale supervised data foundation for cross-platform robot action prediction under the specified offline protocol.

Future benchmark extensions should measure data-scaling behavior, held-out-platform transfer, additional policy families, closed-loop execution, and the effect of event-derived supervision on real-robot success. These evaluations are required to isolate the dataset factors that govern policy scaling and generalization in open-world manipulation.
